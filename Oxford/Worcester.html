The buildings of Worcester College are diverse, especially in the main quadrangle. Looking down into the main quadrangle from the entrance through the main building, to the right is an imposing eighteenth century building in the neo-classical style; and to the left a row of medieval buildings known as "the cottages", which are among the oldest residential buildings in Oxford. These cottages are the most substantial surviving part of Gloucester College, Worcester's predecessor on the same site. The chapel is highly unusual and decorative, being predominantly pink. The pews are decorated with carved animals, including kangaroos and whales, and the walls are riotously colourful, and include frescoes of dodos and peacocks.

Worcester College settles into the night with a quieter dignity than Balliol ever manages. The stone here feels more patient and less judgmental. Its walls have learned how to keep secrets without needing to posture about it. The quad is dimly lit with lamplight that reflects softly off of clipped hedges and the dark surface of the canal beyond. The air is cool and damp with the promise of fog later in the evening. Within one of the older buildings, away from student traffic and administrative eyes, a set of rooms has been repurposed in ways that the prospectus does not mention. A broad oak table, scarred by use, sits in the centre of this one, surrounded by several chairs that do not quite match. The walls are lined with shelves that are stocked with a careful mix of academic texts and esoteric volumes and a laptop hums softly at one end of the table. Its screen casts a pale, cold light that sharply contrasts with the warm glow of a shaded desk lamp. Seated in front of the laptop is Dr Marcus Bell of the&nbsp;Ordo Dracul.
