The crane looms over the spaceport like a tired animal forced back onto its feet. Its long arm is locked in a slightly off-centre cant, the servos are whining just a fraction too high and a vibration that never quite settles is running through the frame. Up close, the illusion of functionality falls apart quickly. The first thing that stands out is the heat. One of the primary actuator housings is running far hotter than it should be, enough that the durasteel plating has begun to discolour. It isn't catastrophic yet, but it’s been compensating for something for a while. Upon a closer look, the reason is obvious. Two of the load-balancing subroutines are offline, their status lights dark, forcing the crane to shove mass distribution onto a single overworked stabiliser. Tracing the issue deeper reveals a mess that isn’t just wear and tear. Inside the maintenance access panel, the wiring loom has been patched and re-patched, splices stacked on splices, insulation stripped back and replaced with incompatible polymer sheathing. One cable in particular, an old Republic-standard control line, has been jury-rigged to interface with a much newer firmware module. The connection technically works, but every command sent through it is arriving half a tick late, causing constant micro-corrections. That delay is what’s shaking the crane apart. Worse, the diagnostic buffer is full of suppressed error flags. Someone didn’t fix this problem. They told the crane to stop reporting it. A crude override buried in the code is forcing the system to ignore fault thresholds and keep operating unless a total failure occurs. That explains why the crane hasn’t shut itself down... and why, when it does fail, it will do so violently. Until the control cable has been properly replaced, the fault-suppression override has been removed and the balancing subroutines have been brought back online and recalibrated, the crane can still lift, but every load increases the risk of a sudden seizure, dropped cargo, or a full arm-lock at the worst possible moment. This isn’t a mystery problem. It’s a known problem that somebody chose to ignore.

Vekk's office isn’t really an office, so much as a box that somebody once intended to be temporary and then never bothered to improve. Corrugated plasteel walls sweat heat, their seams stained with old oil and newer fingerprints. A single oscillating fan rattles overhead, doing more noise than work. The air smells of hot circuitry, cheap caf and stressed Bothan. Juke finds Vekk half-turned away, braced over a crate that’s been repurposed into a desk. Three holoscreens float above it at mismatched angles, each scrolling different problems. There's an incoming scrap tally, labour assignments blinking red and a persistent alert tagged BAY 27 – DELAY (PRIORITY) that Vekk keeps swiping aside with increasing irritation. The Bothan’s ears twitch the moment that Juke enters. One swivels back first, then the other, before Vekk finally turns, his dark eyes sharp and calculating. "Well?" Vekk asks, his voice clipped. "Tell me you’ve got good news, or at least news that isn’t about something else falling apart." He listens as Juke reports in, his expression shifting in small, precise ways. An ear flattens at the mention of the crane and he exhales sharply through his nose when the error flags come up. By the time the warning about Bay 27 lands, Vekk's fingers are drumming against the crate, claws clicking in a nervous rhythm. "Of course it's Bay 27," he mutters. "Everything expensive goes through 27." Vekk straightens and jabs a clawed finger at one of the holoscreens, enlarging a schematic of the crane and its load tolerances. Red lines spider across the display where margins have been exceeded. "You did what you could," he grudgingly, but sincerely recognises. "Physical fixes, error flags restored... that buys us time. Not safety. Time." He glances up at Juke and gives a short nod. "That's worth full pay." Vekk taps his console, and a soft chime confirms the transfer. He flicks a small credit chit across the crate without ceremony. "Extra, too," he adds, almost as an afterthought. "For not pretending that it was fine and letting me find out the loud way." One of his ears cocks toward the open doorway as a distant metallic groan echoes through the yard. The fan rattles harder for a moment. "I’ll divert overflow away from the crane if I can," Vekk continues, already half-turned back to his screens. "Survey Corps won’t like it, but they'll like a collapsed cargo hook even less." He pauses and glances back over his shoulder. "If anyone asks," Vekk says dryly, "I was never warned. Understood?" The corner of his mouth twitches in something that is half a smile and half a grimace, before he waves Juke off with a flick of his paw and turns back to managing the yard. (edited)
