Team Cresh isn't hard to spot. The assigned cargo sled is a big, battered repulsor pallet marked CRESH–7 in flaking blue stencil. As Dru approaches it, he finds four workers already there. Three are exactly what he expected and one is absolutely not. The first is a wiry human man in his early twenties, with a sunburned nose and jittery nerves. His vest is three sizes too big and he keeps tugging at it, as though he’s afraid that it might explode. The moment that Dru nears, the kid gives him a panicked thumbs-up, before nearly dropping the datapad that he’s holding. The second is a broad-shouldered Devaronian, whose horns have been filed blunt. He's lounging against the side of the sled like the galaxy owes him money, chewing a stimstick with bored aggression. His arms are knotted with muscle and he sizes Dru up the way that predators size up competition. The third is a pale Mirialan woman with a loading harness strapped tight across her shoulders. Her facial tattoos look ceremonial, but her expression is very much not. She has the thousand-meter stare of someone who's been on shift twelve hours too long and acknowledges Dru with the smallest of nods, before returning to calibrating the sled's clamps. Then there’s the last worker, who is a human child of, maybe, nine years old. He's perched on a crate like a feral alley-cat, kicking his heels against the metal. He wears oversized goggles and a toolbelt that looks as though it might have been stolen from somebody's apprentice. The kid's entire job appears to be getting in the way and the moment that he sees Dru, he brightens. "You’re the new big guy!" He declares, voice cracking halfway through the sentence. "I'm supposed to watch for, uh, 'dangerous elements'. That's what Chief Cresh said. You look like one," he adds, puffing himself up. "Force save us," the Devaronian mutters from behind the boy. The Mirialan taps a command into the sled and it floats a few centimetres higher, humming steady and strong. The cargo that they're meant to move sits stacked on a nearby pallet lift. The durasteel crates are stamped with the bay's manifest code 77-BLUE. It's not the locked-down stuff, but it’s still heavy enough to break bones if somebody gets sloppy. The shift is already in motion with dockhands shouting, sleds humming and, somewhere overhead, warning klaxons blare as a shuttle reverses thrusters. "You take the forward stabiliser. Try not to let the kid under the sled. He does that sometimes," the Mirialan tells Dru, her voice low and even, while the child beams proudly.

The team shifts as Dru steps into place and the jittery human gives him a nervous thumbs-up again, this time managing not to drop the datapad that he’s clutching, although his hands still shake as though the thing weighs a ton. The Devaronian snorts, but steps aside enough to give Dru space, sizing him up with a low grunt. The Mirialan woman, worn thin by exhaustion, gives Dru a curt nod. The kid, on the other hand, immediately brightens up. "Right! No pancakes. Got it," he salutes with all the confidence of somebody who does not actually have it. Then, he trots off to a stack of crates to keep lookout, a little too proud of his assigned role. Dru's station is immediately clear. A battered old cargo lift platform sits half-raised and groaning under a cluster of misaligned crates, its antigravs flickering and one stabilizer flashing FAULT. The Cathar chief wasn’t kidding. One wrong jolt and something's coming down. As Dru steps into position, the Mirialan taps a control panel. The lift shudders, lets out a mechanical whine and grudgingly rises just enough for Dru to get leverage under the first crate. Heat rolls off of the machinery and fuel vapour hangs in the air like a permanent haze. Somewhere deeper in the bay, a klaxon bleats and metal screams against metal. The others begin to shift their footing when Dru moves, watching how he handles the crate and following his rhythm. Then, the lift gives another troubling shudder. "Uhh, it’s doing the thing again!" The kid calls out, pointing from his position perched atop a crate-stack like a scruffy little lookout gremlin.

Dru's boots bite into the duracrete as he leans back on the sled handle, using his weight like a counterbalance. The overloaded hover-platform shrieks in protest, letting out a high, metallic keening that makes half the bay flinch and look over. For a moment, it looks as though the entire thing is about to pitch sideways and go crashing to the floor, but then the centre of gravity shifts. The stabiliser on the left gasps, spitting sparks, and the other three catch the load a half-second later. The hover-field flickers, surges and finally evens out, hovering low, but level. The crates rattle dangerously, but stay put, and a wave of shuddering mechanical relief runs through the sled. "It worked! It worked! Keep it there, big guy!" The kid exclaims from his lookout perch with a whoop. The Devaronian gives Dru a grudgingly impressed grunt and the Mirialan woman wipes sweat from her forehead, before shooting Dru a nod. The failing stabiliser continues to spit the occasional warning flicker. It’s not fixed, not by a long shot, but Dru's improvised counterbalance has bought them precious seconds. Enough to move and avoid a catastrophic crash. In Bay 7, not dying and not ruining the cargo ranks as exceptional work. However, before anybody can congratulate him properly, a voice crackles over the bay-wide tannoy. It sounds sharp, harried and unmistakably angry. "Security detail for Cargo Lot Seven, shift leads report: The Republic Survey Corps shuttle in Bay Twenty-Seven is behind schedule again. If I find out that any of you have been messing with their manifests, spirits help you." The workers around Dru exchange glances. With the sled now barely stable, the rest of the crew begins pushing forwards again, toward the row of bays that leads deeper into the facility. The hum of the failing stabiliser becomes a nervous heartbeat beneath the crates. Bay 27 isn’t far and they'll be rolling right past it.

The announcement echoes throughout the bay on crackling speakers, the words half-lost in the industrial din, but 'Bay 27' carries well enough. Even before the sled's course angles closer, the difference is visible. Bay 27 is louder, tighter and more controlled than the surrounding docks. Temporary barricades of duraplast and cargo pallets have been set up to funnel foot traffic, forcing workers and bystanders alike into narrow corridors watched by Republic security. These are militia auxiliaries, rather than soldiers, but they're armed and alert. The cargo itself sits in neat, regimented stacks, in sharp contrast to the usual chaos of the port. Standardized gray Survey Corps crates with hazard striping and serials stenciled in precise Aurebesh. Some are marked with atmospheric warnings and others with sealed-environment icons. A few bear reinforced locks and Republic seals that look freshly applied, their adhesive still catching the light. A shuttle squats on the pad, engines idling with a low, persistent thrum that vibrates faintly through the duracrete. Its ramp is down, but loading has stalled. Workers are arguing with a harried-looking officer, while a pair of technicians wrestle with a malfunctioning grav-loader nearby. Tight shoulders, raised voices and glances at chronos ticking down make the delay painfully obvious. As Dru's sled rattles past the edge of the bay, one of the auxiliaries watches it closely, his hand resting near his blaster and his eyes flicking between the unstable load and the workers guiding it. Another marks something on a datapad, lips pressed thin. Everyone looks like they’re waiting for the next thing to go wrong. Above it all, the engines' steady thrum syncs with the protesting whine of Dru’s sled. Two failing systems straining under pressure.

The noise in Bay 27 is layered with clipped orders being barked over commlinks, the hiss of pressure seals cycling and the metallic clatter of cargo being hastily re-stacked, all over the top of the steady industrial rhythm of the spaceport. Somewhere overhead, a warning klaxon pulses at low volume. The space itself has been partially reconfigured on the fly. Portable barrier posts and luminescent guide-strips have been deployed to funnel foot traffic away from the central loading zone, creating narrow corridors that feel uncomfortably tight for anything heavier than a handcart. Dockworkers and freelancers are being waved through by Republic security in piecemeal fashion, their movements checked and redirected with visible impatience. At the heart of the bay sits the reason for it all. A long, low transport shuttle squats on its landing struts, its hull marked with the faded sigil of the Republic Survey Corps. Its engines are cold, but not quiet, with auxiliary systems thrumming unevenly. It sounds like machinery running on borrowed time. Irregularly-shaped cargo pallets are stacked nearby under temporary tarps. Some of the cases are long or reinforced crates, or sensor housings wrapped in shock mesh. It all looks too standardised and careful be civilian freight. Republic militia are everywhere, but not in parade formation. These are working guards with helmets unclasped and rifles slung, but close at hand, eyes constantly moving. They watch the crowd, the cargo and each other. The delay has frayed nerves, and it shows in the way that hands rest just a little too close to triggers. As Dru's sled approaches, its protesting stabilisers draw attention immediately and a few heads snap around. One guard lifts a hand, then hesitates, reassessing when he sees the load's markings and the improvised team pushing it along the approved path. The corridor feels narrower now, with bodies pressed back against barriers and workers stepping aside with a mix of annoyance and relief. Above it all, the bay's overhead gantry cranes sit idle, locked in place like watchful predators. Unused, but ready.
